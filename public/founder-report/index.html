Contents & Editorial
By Ian Browne · for founders

This is the version without the HR voice.

On 6 May 2026 we ran the Techstars Belfast Community Development Lab at Ormeau Labs. Fifty-odd people. Founders, investors, programme operators, government, universities, a few bankers, a couple of angels. Eight tables. Four themed sprints. Granola transcribed every conversation.

The transcripts are anonymised. The quotes are verbatim. What founders said in that room is not what the institutional conversation about Northern Ireland's startup economy currently says. That gap is the most useful finding in this document, and it is mostly about you.

This report is the founder-to-founder version of the conversation that actually happened at the bar after the event ended. The bracing version. Universities, government, investors, and corporates can read it. They will learn things. Some of those things will be uncomfortable. Some will be useful. That's their problem. The audience is you.

Three things to know. First: founders prefaced almost every critique with gratitude — this report keeps that tone where it earns it and drops it where it doesn't. Second: all names are removed; voices stand by their roles. Third: pour a coffee. Twenty minutes.

What this report is NOT
  • A policy submission.
  • A critique of any single agency.
  • A balanced view.
  • A document calibrated for a Permanent Secretary.

It is a field report from your peers. Use it accordingly.

What everyone agreed on
01
The room agreed on more than you'd think
unanimous · five things

Before we get to the bracing parts, the easy one. Five things every table agreed on:

  • Capital here is too local.
  • The support landscape is too crowded to navigate.
  • AI has already changed what one person can build.
  • Belfast founders are calibrating against the wrong people.
  • The community is genuinely generous — and that is rare.
We're all here with the same intention. We want to make this place better. We want to make it easier to start a company. — The whole room, in different words

Half the room also said versions of "and the system isn't keeping up." Both things are true at the same time. The rest of this report is about what to do about it.

If you're here for reassurance, the next section is for you — five things that are genuinely working. If you're here for honesty, skip to Section 3. Both are useful. The order doesn't matter.

techstarsSection 02 · What's actually working
You are not building in a broken place
02
What's actually working
six bright spots · use all of them

You are not building in a broken place. You are building in a place that has not yet learned how good it can be.

2.1   The community is unusually generous

Help is offered here without immediate transactional cost. Warm intros open doors. Coffee meetings are not yet priced. In London this stopped being true around 2018. In Dublin around 2021. In Belfast it is still true, and it is the single most underrated asset you have.

What you do: use it. Pay it forward. It has a half-life.

2.2   A new generation is operating at a different gear

AI-native solo founders. Domain experts who spent ten years inside government, finance, law, or property, now building products in those domains. Bootstrapped operators winning international contracts before they've engaged with a single local agency. The cohort emerging now is not the previous generation.

What you do: recognise it. Operate at that gear. The unit economics have changed.

2.3   The infrastructure is here

Walkable density between Ormeau Labs, Catalyst, Innovation Centres, Eagle Labs, Ulster and Queen's. A cost base that still meaningfully advantages you against London or Dublin. Talent that stays longer than it would elsewhere. Strong technical graduates from both universities.

What you do: stop building from scratch. Operate the ecosystem you have at a higher gear.

2.4   Specific programmes are doing real work

Founder Labs (the NY trip cited as the unlock) · Ormeau Labs / Eagle Labs (default front door) · Catalyst Invent (where multiple founders started) · Awakened Angels (small cheques to women founders) · Go Succeed (broad mentoring, regional reach) · Intertrade Ireland (cross-border, underused).

What you do: use the programmes that work. Route around the rest.

Without one or two key people opening doors, I'd still be at this trying to figure out the basics. — Founder, in the room

2.5   The honesty in the room

The asset you don't appreciate yet. Most ecosystems can't run a workshop like 6 May without it collapsing into self-promotion. Belfast can. Investors said publicly that NI multiples are coming down. Programme operators acknowledged silos. Founders openly said they would raise outside NI.

What you do: be honest yourself. You're surrounded by people who can take it.

2.6   There is local proof you don't need to leave

Cloudsmith.
Built in Belfast · Sold globally from day one · Series C from international capital · Did not relocate.
Won by being the global category leader in their niche while based in Northern Ireland. The argument that you can't build something globally significant from here is now empirically untrue. The proof is local, recent, and accessible.

What you do: stop using geography as a reason. The benchmark is global category leadership. The geography is no longer a constraint. The ambition is.

Things to be honest with yourself about
part one of two · read twice

3.1   You are probably benchmarking against the wrong people

This was the most important thing nobody quite said out loud. Your peer group sets your pace. If everyone around you is running a six-minute mile and clapping, you'll run a six-minute mile and feel like you're winning. You're not. You're the fastest runner in a slow race.

This is not a problem of risk tolerance. The founders in that room work harder, with less, than founders in cities I've worked across. It is a problem of benchmark. Belfast founders are calibrating against the company down the road. The right benchmark is the global category leader in whatever niche you've chosen — wherever they happen to be based.

This is not abstract. Cloudsmith did it locally and didn't move. The benchmark is reachable. The question is whether you are willing to set it.

What you do this week

Pick the global category leader in your niche. Know their unit economics, their pricing, their hiring pace, their churn, their distribution. Make them your reference point, not the company down the road. If you can't name them, you don't know your category well enough yet.

3.2   You are probably chasing grants you should be ignoring

Grants are not free money. They are slow money with strings. Founders described becoming "grant monkeys" — chasing grants as a substitute for chasing customers. POC grants that won't pay for the £200/month of AI tooling that would actually accelerate you, but will pay for a £100k developer you don't really need.

What you do this week

Use small grants tactically for market validation. Stop tailoring your business plan to chase application criteria. The cheapest, fastest, most validating money is a paying customer. If you can't get customers, more grant money won't fix that. It will postpone the moment you find out.
If you're a founder with big ambition then the opportunities exist outside of Northern Ireland. I wouldn't benchmark myself against that calibre. I want to find out. And I'm not going to find out here. — Founder · on local benchmarks

More honesty · the structural critique
capital ceiling · industrial-policy gap · network trap

4.1   The local capital ceiling is real

Founders in the room openly stated they will raise outside Northern Ireland. NI pre-seed cheques are smaller than the equivalent in Dublin and meaningfully smaller than London. Local term sheets sometimes contain structures — equity ratchets, board-seat concessions — that wouldn't be accepted in a more mature market.

This is not a critique of local investors. They have the cheque sizes they have. It is a critique of the founder behaviour that defaults to them first.

What you do this week

Plan for external capital from week one. Build the deck for London, Dublin, New York, or San Francisco. The local funds will see you anyway if you're good. £50 Ryanair ticket. Run the fundraising process at the level of the global capital market. Don't take the first cheque if it comes with 50% board seats.

4.2   The system around you doesn't have an industrial policy for founders

This is the structural critique. Read it carefully.

The instinct when you hit institutional friction is to blame the institution. That is the wrong target. The honest finding is upstream of any single agency. Northern Ireland does not yet have an industrial policy that treats the indigenous founder economy as a serious GDP lever. No Programme for Government commitment names it. No departmental target measures it. No coherent set of mandates aligns the bodies that should be working together.

Inside that vacuum, individual agencies do their best with the mandates and KPIs they have. Some — Founder Labs, Catalyst Invent, Intertrade Ireland, BBB's recent engagement — are doing meaningful work. Every friction founders described is a downstream consequence of the same upstream gap: no coordinated economic strategy that treats founders as infrastructure.

This is not the agencies' fault. It is the system's fault. Fixing it requires Stormont-level decisions, not individual form-redesigns.

What you do about it

Don't take institutional friction personally. Don't conclude any one agency is the problem. The problem is upstream of all of them. Route around what doesn't work. Use what does. And recognise that you, as a founder operating here, are part of the evidence base that forces the upstream conversation to happen. This report is one of the things that forces it. Section 8 is the other.

4.3   Your network is your unfair advantage. It is also your trap.

Warm intros open doors here. That is real. Use it. But it creates a small-pond reflex. You'll get reassuring advice from people whose only reference point is also Belfast. You'll feel busy, well-supported, and surrounded — and you'll discover three years later that you didn't move fast enough.

What you do this week

Use the local network for local things — friendship, density, founder-to-founder honesty, warm intros to specific people. Set your benchmark globally. Don't confuse support for validation. Your neighbours might love you. The market doesn't care where you live.
Until the Programme for Government names indigenous founder density as a serious GDP lever, every agency will continue to do its best inside a mandate that doesn't quite fit what early-stage founders need. — The structural finding · author's view
"There's not enough capital in the system. And the capital that does exist isn't proper venture money. My ambition as a founder is almost not to raise money through the local funds. Because there's a cap on that."
— Founder on raising outside NI
"We just have no idea where to go for investment capital. It's terribly signposted. Do we even have any VC firms in Northern Ireland?"
— Early-stage founder

techstarsSection 05 · Speed & AI
The two nobody talks about
05
Speed & AI · the two nobody quite said out loud
your only structural advantage · against incumbents

5.1   Speed is the thing nobody talks about

This came up at multiple tables and almost didn't make the room's official findings — but it is one of the most important.

This is not a moral judgement. Quality of life is a real NI advantage. But you are competing with founders who do not have offices empty by 4pm, and the only competitive advantage you have as a founder against incumbents is speed.

Speed is very poor in Northern Ireland. In the US, you move so much faster. Get an introduction and it happens quickly. Breakfast meetings, evening meetings. There is still too much of a laid-back attitude where offices are empty by 4pm. — Founder, AI-native software

What you do this week

Be the fast one. Take meetings at 8am. Reply within the hour. Ship the prototype in a week. Make every customer interaction visibly faster than they expect. Your competitive advantage isn't capital. It's velocity.

5.2   AI is not a feature you bolt on

The strongest signal from the AI table: founders already using AI properly are operating at a different gear. Founders treating AI as a sticker are losing.

The room also produced honest scepticism, worth preserving: "There's limits to it. We don't have enough data, enough compute for it. There's more supply than what can actually be demanded." Both things are true. AI is not a magic wand. AI as an operating model is a structural advantage. The difference is whether you are using it to do work or to look like you are doing work.

What you do this week

Treat AI as how you run the company, not what the company does. Automate the parts of consultancy, sales, research, customer interviewing, and admin that previously needed a team. Use the saved time to do more customer conversations. Use the saved money to extend runway. If you're a domain expert, your moat is your knowledge plus AI plus speed. None of those three work without the other two.
"If you're still using ChatGPT that's a red flag. The capability of AI has moved way beyond. People in San Francisco are using AI to do the work."
— Founder, AI-native
"I think loads of the time people are trying to just hack it onto a business and it doesn't really give anything actually a benefit."
— Founder on AI-as-sticker
"I'm non-technical. If I go to an agency they want to charge me 100k. ChatGPT helped me write the spec — without that I wouldn't have understood anything."
— Founder, non-technical
"We're trying to be as close to AI-native as possible. Everyone is on Claude, Cowork, doing QA cycles, generating design assets, writing copy. There's no rules — you just decide one morning to do it."
— Founder, AI-native software

Two things are true at the same time. Treat AI like a sticker and it's a waste. Treat it as an operating model and it's a structural advantage. The difference is the next month of your work.

Tactics, not policy
06
What you can actually do this week
tactics not policy · pick five · ship them

Five categories. Pick at least one from each. By Friday.

Capital & fundraising

  • Plan for external capital from week one.
  • Build a target list of 40 investors before you start raising. Stack-rank them.
  • Don't take coffee meetings with VCs between rounds. Send the polite template instead.
  • Refuse term sheets with equity ratchets and 50% board seats.
  • Apply for SEIS assurance early if you're UK-incorporated.
  • Don't max out grants just because you can. Match the grant to the actual work.
  • If a customer offers to pay for an MVP, take the money. Customer cash is the cheapest cash you'll ever raise.

Customers & traction

  • 50 customer interviews. Recorded. Transcribed. Turned into a quotes database. That database is also your fundraising deck.
  • Charge for the MVP. Wait-list conversion is rarely what you think it is.
  • Founder-led sales until you can write the playbook. Then hire.
  • Show up at sector-specific events, not general founder mixers, once you're past the earliest stage.

Network

  • Find the second- and third-time founders in the room. Stay close.
  • Pay back every warm intro within a week with a status update.
  • Refuse the lazy "introduce me to all your investors" ask. Be specific.
  • Build a small circle of four founders you trust. Then four more.
  • When you raise, introduce two founders behind you on the day you announce.

Ambition & pace

  • Get on a plane. London, Dublin, New York, or San Francisco. This quarter, not next.
  • Pick the global category leader in your niche. Study them properly.
  • Stop asking founders down the road how they're doing. Ask founders solving the same problem somewhere bigger.
  • Take the 8am meeting. Reply within the hour. Ship in days.
  • Beat one global competitor on velocity for the rest of the year.

AI

  • Use AI as how you run the company.
  • Automate one workflow this week. Then one more next week.
  • If you're paying an agency £100k for something AI can do in a week, you don't have a tech problem — you have an AI problem.
  • Read a Claude release note before reading another newsletter.
  • Use AI to draft, never to think. The thinking is still yours.

What I'm doing about it & what comes next
Ian's commitments · not the room's findings

Five things I'm doing, in progress

Marked clearly as Ian's commitments, not the room's findings.

  1. A working founder peer network — 200+ NI founders across stages, single channel, where a first-timer can ask a Series A founder a question and get an answer in 20 minutes. Building it now.
  2. Continuous, not occasional, global exposure — routing founders directly into the global Techstars network, US accelerator alumni, and international capital.
  3. Public pressure on the systemic gap — the absence of an industrial policy for the founder economy. This report is the first move.
  4. A cross-referral protocol between Techstars Belfast and partner programmes. Nobody falls through the cracks.
  5. Honest, in-public, founder-led reporting like this one — every six months.

A note on what comes next

This is the first State of the Nation report from the founder economy in Northern Ireland. It will not be the last. The next one is in six months. Either things will have moved or they won't. Both outcomes are useful.

If you are a founder, the doors at Techstars Belfast are open. If you want to be in the next one, come and find us.
