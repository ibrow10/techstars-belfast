Contents & Editorial Note
Editorial

A community reading itself, honestly.

This is a working draft of what was said when sixty people — founders, investors, programme leads, agency teams, university commercialisation officers, ecosystem builders — sat down at eight tables in Belfast and tried to answer one question: what does it actually take to build a company in Northern Ireland, and where is the system letting founders down?

It is structured around the Seven Capitals framework from Brad Feld and Ian Hathaway's The Startup Community Way. The framework forces us to look past the loudest complaint — financial capital — and into the quieter capitals that often decide whether a community compounds or stalls.

Three things to know before you read on. First, founders prefaced almost every critique with gratitude; this report tries to keep that tone. Second, all names of speakers, companies, and third parties have been removed — the voices stand by their roles. Third, this is a draft, not a verdict; the open questions at the back are an invitation to keep the conversation going.

techstarsSection 01 · Headline finding
The surplus & its quiet flaw
01
Headline finding
evidence: high · across all three recordings
The argument in one sentence

NI has a deep surplus of network capital — and a structural deficit of institutional and cultural capital around founder protection.

And the surplus itself has a quiet flaw the ecosystem has not yet named: founders are generous with introductions but inconsistent with follow-through.

In Feld's terms, there is no shortage of warmth, generosity, or willingness to help. The senior operators, the warm-intro culture, the give-first instinct — the network and cultural capitals at their best — are visible everywhere in these conversations.

What is missing is twofold. First, the institutional infrastructure that would protect founders who haven't yet earned access to that warmth: a public best-practice guide, a legible map of the support landscape, governance standards for who can claim to be supporting founders. Second, the cultural discipline that turns warm intros into compounding outcomes — the simple act of following up.

One senior operator estimated that warm intros take him 45 minutes to do well, and that the majority of his introductions never produce a follow-up conversation by the recipient. That is a cultural-capital problem: the surplus the ecosystem rightly prides itself on is being partially wasted at the user end.

The financial-capital complaint that often dominates these conversations elsewhere — "we don't have enough money here" — is present in the NI conversation but blunter than expected. There is enough capital, particularly when you include cross-border SEIS-eligible angel money from Dublin, RoI, and London. The binding constraints are legibility (founders can't find their way into the system) and discipline (founders aren't always making the most of the system once they're in).

The seven capitals at a glance
02
Methodology & coverage map
framework: Feld & Hathaway · 2020

Why the Seven Capitals frame.

When founders complain "there isn't enough capital here," they almost always mean financial capital. But financial is only one of seven. Feld and Hathaway argue the first three (intellectual, human, financial) are the visible inputs — ideas, talent, money. The other four (network, cultural, physical, institutional) are infrastructure running quietly in the background. Often the binding constraint is one of those quieter four.

The frame disciplines the analysis. The temptation when summarising founders is to organise around the loudest complaints; this lens makes us look at the quiet places too. The capitals that aren't being talked about may be the most important finding of all.

Network and culture are where NI's surplus and binding constraint both sit. The other quiet capitals — institutional, physical — are where the deficit hides.

techstarsSection 03 · Network capital
Invisible outside, generous inside
03
Network capital · #network
evidence: high · most-discussed capital

The most discussed capital across all three recordings — and, paradoxically, the one founders are most polarised on. Once inside the network, the experience is uniformly positive: warm, generous, transformational. Getting inside is the hard part. The Northern Ireland ecosystem is, in effect, hidden from outside it.

Three sub-themes sharpened by the data

  1. The "shockwave" gap. London and New York have generations of large successful companies whose people filter out into the ecosystem — the Stripe mafia, the Revolut mafia. NI doesn't have those generations of spinouts yet. Cloudsmith and a small number of others are the first wave. Not a complaint about today; a structural observation about time.
  2. The follow-up culture problem. Founders ask for warm intros and don't act on them. One senior operator: a single intro takes 45 minutes to do well; a significant proportion never produce a follow-up by the founder. Investors confirm it from their side. A cultural-capital deficit dressed as a network-capital surplus.
  3. The peer-group infrastructure gap. Plenty of programmes that bring founders together for finite periods — no persistent peer-to-peer infrastructure where a Series B founder can answer a first-time founder's question in 35 minutes. Lithuania's Unicorn Association is the named comparator.

What's working

  • Warm-introduction culture is strong; senior operators make time.
  • A latent senior-advisor pool — ex-operators winding down, willing to mentor.
  • Real-time give-first behaviour observed inside the conversations themselves.
  • Cohort & alumni networks (Founder Labs, Tech Stars Belfast, Awaken) function inside their boundaries.

Where the friction is

  • No public-facing front door to the ecosystem.
  • Mentor relationships happen by chance, not design.
  • The ecosystem is legible only after entry.
  • Follow-up discipline is poor; the compounding engine throttles itself.
  • No persistent cross-cohort peer infrastructure.
Every single one of my forty-five investments has come through a warm introduction. Never, ever underestimate the power of your network. — Multi-time exited founder, now angel investor
"An introduction might take me 45 minutes to an hour. So there has to be a why. Reality is so many founders don't even follow up with the actors who are begging to make the introduction."
— Senior ecosystem operator

"In bigger cities there's a flywheel. People go through the gears at really successful companies, then spin off and start their own. We don't really have that here yet. We've got a couple of good candidates. That just takes time. You can't rush it."
— Founder, AI-native software
#network#human#observation
"Boulder is 110,000 people. They've produced ten YC companies. Belfast is three times the size — Limerick alone has 10 YC. That shows what's possible if you leverage the network properly."

#network#cultural
Suggestions raised
  • A curated senior-advisor pool, accessed by warm intro not application.
  • Smaller structured peer-cohort sessions over big general events.
  • A persistent NI-wide founder peer group — a Lithuanian-Unicorn-style channel.
  • Cross-cohort meetups across accelerator alumni.
  • Normalise follow-up etiquette in programme onboarding: close the loop within a week.

techstarsSection 04 · Financial capital
The geography penalty
04
Financial capital · #financial
evidence: high · most emotionally charged thread

Three sub-themes from the original pass have strengthened; three more have surfaced. Together they redraw the picture: the binding constraint isn't that there's no money, it's that the money is unevenly priced, awkwardly accessed, and — for a new kind of company — increasingly optional.

  1. The geography penalty. Identical metrics produce materially worse equity terms in Belfast than in London — despite NI's lower burn arguably favouring it on unit economics.
  2. Grants: generous but rigid. Headline ceilings ("up to £35k", "up to £75k") encourage founders to over-ask; one-size criteria don't accommodate non-standard models.
  3. Predatory operators in the advice gap. Ratchet term sheets and 50% board control demands have been seen from local VCs in NI's recent history. Less common today; the warning persists.
  4. The active bootstrap-vs-raise debate. No longer fringe. AI-enabled services founders are deliberately not seeking equity. Angels report the seed-strap model is now more attractive than companies that will raise Series A.
  5. Cross-border capital structure. SEIS opens an investor pool RoI doesn't have; founders are figuring out dual-entity setups individually.
  6. AI is inflating grant-application volume. Same pots, more applications, harder to distinguish signal.

Notes for synthesis

The ratchet quote is publishable but should be framed: it's about NI's recent past, not present-day behaviour. The bootstrap-vs-raise debate is the section that will most divide an INI-stakeholder audience — present it as live and unresolved, with founder voices on both sides.

If you have two businesses with the same metrics, the business in London gets better terms for no other reason than geography. Yet their costs are higher. — Founder, B2B SaaS
"It's always 'up to 35, up to 75' — you don't have to put in 75, but of course you're going to max it out."
— Multi-time founder
#financial#institutional#friction
"The way the investment space has moved is that everything moves towards the VCs that come in at a later stage — they come in with liquidation preferences that screw over the earlier angels. So actually, a company looking to seed-strap and never raise Series A is way more attractive to me as an angel."
— Angel investor
#financial#observation
"If you want money, ask for advice. If you want advice, ask for money. The best time to talk to investors is when you don't need their money."
— Multi-time exited founder, now angel investor
#financial#cultural#suggestion
"They were trying to de-risk the funding by an equity ratchet agreement. If we missed targets they took equity off the founders. They wanted 50% of our board seats."
— Multi-time founder, recounting NI VC experience c. 2019

techstarsSection 05 · Cultural capital
Warmth, follow-up, fear of failure
05
Cultural capital · #cultural
evidence: high · most consequential quiet capital

The cultural undercurrents are the most consequential capital running quietly in the background. The data sharpened three of them.

Three undercurrents

  1. Follow-up as a cultural discipline. A network surplus the ecosystem is partly wasting at the user end. Costs zero to fix; cannot be mandated; can be cultivated.
  2. Fear of failure linked to a sibling observation that technology talent is not respected here as it is in San Francisco or London. Starting a company is socially riskier when (a) failure reads as personal failure, and (b) the path itself is less culturally legitimised than other professional routes.
  3. Bootstrap-as-cultural-statement. A new generation of AI-native founders is making bootstrap a deliberate choice — not a fallback. The angels are increasingly making the same case from the other side. The values of "what success looks like" here are mid-revision.

What's working

  • Give-first culture: warm intros, real-time mentoring.
  • Safe-space dynamics in dedicated communities.
  • Critique prefaced with gratitude — a healthy ecosystem signal.
  • Cross-gender problem-solving on the women-founder question.

Where the friction is

  • Pitch-room demographics don't reflect the markets being addressed.
  • No codified founder-protection norms.
  • Narrative concentration on a small number of names.
  • Hesitance to pay advisors with sweat equity, even when it's the right call.

Notes for synthesis. Cultural capital is hard to write about without sounding abstract. The way to make it concrete is to show specific behaviours — the warm intro offered live, the mentor on a council programme, the run club deliberately created to push back against AI-mediated networking — and let those examples carry the meaning of "culture" without using the word.

How can we keep culture with AI? AI is making everything less human. That's why I started the Run Club — so people can connect, not just be fake on LinkedIn liking each other's posts. — Founder, AI fintech / Startup Runners
"Doesn't exist without that lovely conversation. It's a safe space. You've got male allies — but it's not men at the table saying 'do this, do this, do this.' Because to your point on the panel — it's four males facing you. It's not right."
— Bank-led ecosystem operator
#cultural#network#working
"Technology talent here, I don't think it's respected as much as it is in San Francisco or London. How do we bring that into the education system? How do we make it more acceptable to start a company?"
— Founder, closing read-out
#cultural#human#friction
"Just like a best-practice guide that anyone can lift up. So everyone knows where it is — you can go look at stuff and be like, okay, that's definitely not okay."
— Multi-time founder
#cultural#institutional#suggestion

techstarsSection 06 · Institutional capital
The front door problem
06
Institutional capital · #institutional
evidence: high · INI front-door now corroborated independently

Institutional capital in NI is, by international comparison, substantial. Founders in the room repeatedly acknowledged this. The friction is not in the quantity — it's in the legibility and the front door.

  1. Programme architecture is unmapped from the founder's perspective. Each programme is good; the collective is impossible to navigate from outside.
  2. Grant criteria assume one shape of company — typically equity-track, product-led.
  3. Front-door experiences filter out the wrong founders. The "I just walked away from Invest NI" finding is now corroborated independently across multiple recordings — including from a founder who has been operating successfully in NI for six years and has never gone back.
  4. The connection-between-networks problem. A founder rejected by one programme isn't routinely referred onward.

Notes for synthesis

The Invest NI section is sensitive — they're a key partner. The strongest move is to treat the front-door story as constructive, evidence-based feedback worth acting on. It's delivered by founders who otherwise had warm things to say about NI's institutional generosity; corroborated across tables; and there are willing founders inside the room who can help redesign it. The fact that other founders immediately offered warm introductions to specific INI contacts is a generous detail worth keeping — the ecosystem's relational machinery working around its formal one.

I went to Invest NI a couple of years ago. It was a lot of forms and time. When you're running a business and you're really busy, is it even worth going? I just walked away from it. — Founder, six years operating in NI
"We rang up. He explained, and it was kind of: you need to be a client, you need to go onto this web form. He opens it and it's a big form. That's what puts people off."
— Founder, services-led B2B (already exporting)
#institutional#friction
"One thing we could improve is the connection between the networks. If you apply for a Barclays accelerator and aren't a fit, ideally you'd fall into a catch-all where Founder Labs picks you up — rather than fall by the wayside."
— Founder, closing read-out
#institutional#network#suggestion
What's working
  • Quantum & diversity of pathways: councils, INI, university routes, all-island agencies (InterTrade Ireland), British Business Bank's 200+ delivery partners.
  • Founder Labs accelerator pipeline reaching ~100 founders per cycle.
  • Substantive engagement once founders are inside the institutional relationship.

techstarsSection 07 · Human capital
The missing founder mafia
07
Human capital · #human
evidence: high

A consistent message: capability is being acquired through chance, not design. The right mentor, the right programme, the right warm intro — these are still the dominant transmission mechanisms.

Four lenses

  1. The skills gap — negotiation as a missing capability that founders without accelerator access never fully acquire.
  2. The diversity question — women founders navigating an environment built for someone else; the closing read-out surfaced the founder-question asymmetry ("who's the CTO?" — looking for a man even when a woman is the CTO).
  3. The pre-ecosystem gap — a structural absence between standard education paths and the moment a person realises they could build a company. Fear of failure independently flagged as a top-three issue at one of the unrecorded tables.
  4. The "founder mafia" generational gap — London/NY/SF have generations of large successful companies whose alumni become the next generation's founders, advisors, angels. NI doesn't have this yet. The strategic implication: this is a time problem, not a programme problem.

What's working

  • Women-only founder communities producing real outcomes.
  • Bank- and corporate-led programmes emerging (Awaken Hub, Awaken Angels, Eagle Lab).
  • Life-sciences university pipeline producing balanced gender representation in spinouts.
  • AI as an upskilling path for non-technical founders — a chartered accountant building her own EMI scheme.

Where the friction is

  • Negotiation acquired by chance, not design.
  • Investment panels demographically narrow.
  • Few female angels and VCs to draw from.
  • No structured exposure to founder as a viable path at school or undergraduate stage.
  • Question-asymmetry on investment panels — a panel-training issue, not just a representation issue.
We do have talent here, but we haven't got generations of people coming out of massive companies — the Stripe mafia, the Revolut mafia. We haven't got there yet. We have some damn good candidates. — Founder, closing read-out
"I built the EMI scheme myself. A year ago I would have said I'm not technical — but I've used AI to learn how to code. I understand architecture now."
— Founder, AI-native fintech

"I work with a lot of female founders just starting that journey, and they're not getting the investment. They get asked different questions — they get grilled on different things. A lot of them get asked 'who's the CTO?' I'm the CTO. They're looking for the man."

"There's a big gap between going to school, going to university, learning your trade, and then getting to here. It was actually a mentor on a council programme who said, 'you can hire someone to build your tech.' That got me into building tech."

techstarsSection 08 · Intellectual capital
What AI just changed
08
Intellectual capital · #intellectual
evidence: medium-high · live debate

A live and visible tension has emerged here, and we publish it as a tension rather than smooth it over. Both speakers below are credible. Both are right within their frame. The community has not yet built shared language to distinguish them — and naming that gap is this report's contribution.

Position A · Investor
"Consultancy businesses are not investable, full stop. Nobody will ever invest in a consultancy."
— Multi-time exited founder, now angel investor
Position B · Founder
"As one person I can have six clients, when before that would have needed a team of thirty or forty. The cap on what you can earn isn't quite the way it used to be."
— Founder, AI-native consultancy · £400k revenue, week 5

The angel's position reflects the historical model: linear time-for-money. The founder's reflects what AI has changed: productised services with software-like margins. The ecosystem has not yet developed the language to distinguish "old-style consultancy" from "AI-native services". That language gap is itself the intellectual-capital observation.

The moat conversation is genuinely live

Investors and founders are working different definitions of defensibility in real time — proprietary data, embedded workflow, UX, regulatory positioning. None of the speakers were satisfied with the answer. The healthy ecosystem response is to support live thinking — peer fora, founder-investor cross-talk — rather than producing premature canonical answers.

"Just having AI functionality in your product is no longer enough. You need a real data moat — proprietary data, something hard for someone else to acquire."
— VC investor
#intellectual#financial
"There's no such thing as a data moat anymore — all that data is going into the foundation models. The moat now is workflow embedding, UX, and how connected your tool is to your user's actual work."
— Founder (responding directly)
#intellectual
"Six months ago you had assumptions about how sticky a product was. Then OpenAI or Anthropic releases an update and the businesses you thought had solid fundamentals are totally redundant. The pace of change is at an extreme level."
— VC investor
#intellectual#observation
"Anything we can automate in our process of talking to clients, we do. As one person I can have six clients. I find software services companies are super slow to catch up with that."
— Founder, AI-enabled services
#intellectual#working
"The software is the easy bit now. The hard bit is getting in front of the customer."
— Founder

techstarsSection 09 · Physical capital
The absence is the data
09
Physical capital · #physical
evidence: minimal · finding is firm

For three full hours of recorded conversation, no founder talked about Belfast as a place. That's not because the place isn't an asset — it's because the place is taken for granted. A startup community that has stopped seeing its own backdrop has lost a narrative tool.

Founders did not talk about Belfast as a place, didn't reference physical hubs as differentiators, didn't discuss density or co-location as advantages — even when prompted by a facilitator who explicitly raised "density" as a feature of NI.

The closest physical-capital moment was incidental: a founder mentioning they chose Belfast over Dublin because it was "a lot more affordable than Dublin". A quiet positive — surfaced as one sentence, in passing, not as a frame.

Feld and Hathaway specifically argue that physical capital — quality of place, density, infrastructure — runs in the background of the most successful startup communities and is often underestimated. Boulder's identity as a place is, in their writing, inseparable from its identity as a startup community.

What the absence could mean

  • Physical capital genuinely isn't a constraint or differentiator — in which case it's also not a story to tell.
  • Physical capital is taken for granted — in which case it's working but undervalued as narrative.
  • Founders here haven't yet learned to use place as part of their story — in which case there's a missed narrative opportunity.

The third hypothesis is the most useful. Belfast has real physical-capital assets — Ormeau Labs, Catalyst Inc, university corridors, compactness, affordability vs Dublin / London — that are not being deployed as part of NI's startup-community story.

What to do about it

Deliberately surface physical-capital narratives in ecosystem storytelling: affordability vs Dublin, compactness as networking advantage, hub density (Ormeau, Catalyst, university corridors), ease of cross-border travel and work.

techstarsSection 10 · Cross-cutting
Asymmetry & the connected core
10
Cross-cutting observations
patterns running across capitals

The meta-finding: information asymmetry between connected and unconnected founders.

Almost every friction in the dataset can be reframed as an asymmetry problem. The ecosystem's strongest interventions all close this asymmetry for founders inside them. The opportunity is to extend the same protection to founders outside.

Eight patterns that run across capitals

  1. Asymmetry is the meta-finding. Connected founders compound; unconnected ones get filtered out.
  2. NI is rich in two capitals (network, cultural-warmth) and short in two (institutional-legibility, codified culture). The leverage move is to spend the surplus on the deficit.
  3. Founders trust each other more than formal channels. Formal infrastructure sets the conditions; the value transfer happens between founders.
  4. The most experienced founders deliberately limit their exposure to "general" ecosystem activity — small, trusted, online over big, broad, in-person.
  5. Tone: gratitude with honest critique. The ecosystem is being asked to finish the job, not torn down.
  6. The follow-up culture problem — costs zero to fix at founder level. Cannot be mandated; can be cultivated.
  7. The bootstrap-vs-raise debate is no longer fringe. Treat it as live.
  8. The shockwave is yet to come, but the candidates are visible. Cultivate exit alumni before they dissolve into private life.
Cultivate exit alumni before they dissolve into private life. That is the work that produces the next generation of founders, advisors, and angels. — Cross-cutting observation

Open questions & what happens next
an invitation, not a verdict

Eleven questions this draft raises but doesn't resolve.

  1. Who would own the public "founder best practice" guide? Founder Labs? Catalyst? Invest NI? A founder-collective? Awaken Hub? The answer matters because the guide's credibility is in who writes it.
  2. Is the "geography penalty" a fact, a perception, or both? Worth digging into actual term-sheet data alongside founder testimony before publishing as fact.
  3. Why are women-founder programmes so infrequent? Funding constraint, demand misjudgement, or operator capacity? "She Ventures" runs every two years explicitly because of operational lift — that suggests capacity, not demand.
  4. Why is physical capital so absent from these conversations? Now firm finding across three recordings: taken for granted, not viewed as narrative asset.
  5. What's the right unit of "ecosystem map"? Geographic, stage-based, sector-based, founder-type-based?
  6. How replicable is the AI-enabled services model? A small handful of unusually capable founders, or a structural shift in what "scalable" means?
  7. What do founders we didn't hear from think? The sharpest critique would come from founders who never made it in — or who left NI to build elsewhere.
  8. Is there a "tier 2" ecosystem city playbook? Limerick (population 100k, 10 YC companies) is the comparator that most demands attention.
  9. Who would convene the persistent founder peer infrastructure? Catalyst, Founder Labs, and Ormeau Labs all have a credible claim.
  10. What does "investable AI-native services" look like to a sophisticated investor? The "consultancy is not investable / I'm at £400k week five" tension is the biggest unresolved intellectual-capital question. A fireside was offered live at the table — honour the offer.
  11. What is the actual rate of warm-intro follow-up by NI founders, and how does it compare? Empirically. Senior operators perceive the rate as low. Worth measuring.
Live offers made on the record
  • A fireside chat between an angel investor and an AI-native services founder, on the record.
  • Warm introductions to specific INI contacts to fix the front-door experience.
  • A follow-up coffee on machine learning between founders.

These are commitments the ecosystem made to itself, in public. The next pass of this report will track which ones happened.

The ecosystem is not being attacked here. It's being asked to finish the job. — Editorial close
