Contents & Editorial Note
Editorial · by Ian Browne

In better shape than its founders realise. In worse shape than its institutions believe.

On 6 May 2026, fifty-odd people from five stakeholder groups — founders, universities, government, investors, corporates — sat down at eight tables in Belfast for four hours. The Community Development Lab was the first ecosystem-wide listening exercise of its kind in Northern Ireland. Every table was recorded, transcribed, and anonymised. This is what was said.

The argument of the report is structural: indigenous founder density is the conversion mechanism between AI-era productivity and NI's GDP. Northern Ireland's economy is structurally exposed to FDI workflow automation — KYC, AML, legal ops, customer support, BPO. Whether AI grows the local economy or shrinks it depends on how many founders we successfully form, fund, and scale here. That is the mission this report serves.

It is built on the Seven Capitals framework from Feld & Hathaway's The Startup Community Way, with one additional lens: what does each stakeholder group need to do next? The framework forces us past the loudest complaint — financial capital — into the quieter capitals that often decide whether a community compounds or stalls. The stakeholder lens forces specificity. Both are answers to the same question: where is the leverage?

Three things to know before you read on. First, founders prefaced almost every critique with gratitude; this report tries to keep that tone. Second, all names of speakers, companies, and third parties have been removed; voices stand by their roles. Third, this is a working draft, not a verdict — the open offers at the back are an invitation to keep the conversation going.

techstarsSection 01 · Headline finding
The surplus & its quiet flaw
01
Headline finding
evidence: high · across all three recordings
The argument in one sentence

NI's founder economy is in better shape than its founders realise — and in worse shape than its institutions believe.

The cultural raw material is here. The structural layer around it has not kept pace with the technology shift now underway. The window to close that gap is shorter than the system is currently behaving as if it is.

In Feld's terms, NI has a deep surplus of network and cultural capital — the warm-intro culture, the give-first instinct, the senior operators making time — and a structural deficit of institutional legibility and codified founder protection. That much was visible in the room.

The mission this report serves is more specific than "improve the ecosystem." It is using indigenous founder density to raise NI's GDP. Northern Ireland's economy is structurally exposed to FDI workflow automation — KYC, AML, legal ops, customer support, BPO — that current-generation AI agents are now demonstrably replacing. Whether the AI productivity dividend grows the local economy or shrinks it depends on whether we successfully form, fund, and scale founders here. Every founder that succeeds is a small redistribution of that dividend into the local tax base, employment base, and supply chain. The aggregate is GDP movement.

The financial-capital complaint that often dominates these conversations elsewhere — "we don't have enough money here" — is present in the NI conversation but blunter than expected. Per capita, NI has one of the most heavily-funded founder support systems in the UK. The binding constraints are not quantity. They are legibility (founders can't navigate the system), velocity (institutional pace doesn't match founder pace), and ambition (founders benchmark against the company down the road, not the Series A in Palo Alto).

Limerick — one third the size of Belfast — has produced ten YC companies. We have not. The difference is not resources. It is system design and which conversations founders are having.

Methodology & coverage map
framework: Feld & Hathaway · 2020

Why the Seven Capitals frame.

When founders complain "there isn't enough capital here," they almost always mean financial capital. But financial is only one of seven. Feld and Hathaway argue the first three (intellectual, human, financial) are the visible inputs — ideas, talent, money. The other four (network, cultural, physical, institutional) are infrastructure running quietly in the background. Often the binding constraint is one of those quieter four.

The frame disciplines the analysis. The temptation when summarising founders is to organise around the loudest complaints; this lens makes us look at the quiet places too. The capitals that aren't being talked about may be the most important finding of all.

Network and culture are where NI's surplus and binding constraint both sit. The other quiet capitals — institutional, physical — are where the deficit hides.

techstarsSection 03 · Network capital
Invisible outside, generous inside
03
Network capital · #network
evidence: high · most-discussed capital

The most discussed capital across all three recordings — and, paradoxically, the one founders are most polarised on. Once inside the network, the experience is uniformly positive: warm, generous, transformational. Getting inside is the hard part. The Northern Ireland ecosystem is, in effect, hidden from outside it.

Four sub-themes sharpened by the data

  1. The "shockwave" gap. London and New York have generations of large successful companies whose people spin out into new ones — the Stripe mafia, the Revolut mafia. NI doesn't have those generations yet. Cloudsmith and a small number of others are the first wave. A structural observation about time.
  2. The follow-up culture problem. Founders ask for warm intros and don't act on them. A single good intro takes 45 minutes; a significant proportion never produce a follow-up by the founder. A cultural-capital deficit dressed as a network-capital surplus.
  3. Pace. Founders work in weekly sprints. The institutional layer works in quarters; decisions wait on annual leave. The fastest-moving founders in the room have stopped trying to operate at local speed.
  4. Diaspora and the missing peer infrastructure. NI has a globally distributed diaspora it isn't using. Limerick is. And inside NI there is no persistent cross-cohort founder channel where a Series B founder can answer a first-time founder's question in 35 minutes.

What's working

  • Warm-introduction culture is strong; senior operators make time.
  • A latent senior-advisor pool — ex-operators winding down, willing to mentor.
  • Real-time give-first behaviour observed inside the conversations themselves.
  • Cohort & alumni networks (Founder Labs, Tech Stars Belfast, Awaken) function inside their boundaries.

Where the friction is

  • No public-facing front door to the ecosystem.
  • Mentor relationships happen by chance, not design.
  • The ecosystem is legible only after entry.
  • Follow-up discipline is poor; the compounding engine throttles itself.
  • No persistent cross-cohort peer infrastructure.
Every single one of my forty-five investments has come through a warm introduction. Never, ever underestimate the power of your network. — Multi-time exited founder, now angel investor

Suggestions raised

  • A persistent NI-wide founder peer group — a working channel of 200+ founders across stages.

  • Normalise follow-up etiquette in programme onboarding: close the loop within a week.

techstarsSection 04 · Financial capital
The geography penalty
04
Financial capital · #financial
evidence: high · most emotionally charged thread

Per capita, NI has one of the most heavily funded founder-support systems in the UK. The binding constraint isn't quantity. It is design: capital priced for the wrong ambition, grants that don't pay for AI tooling, and match-funding that filters out anyone without personal capital.

  1. The local ceiling is set too low. Identical metrics produce materially worse equity terms in Belfast than in London. Founders openly state they plan to raise outside NI; ambitious founders are not benchmarking themselves against local funds.
  2. Grant design produces "grant monkeys." POC and concept grants are too narrow — they pay for a developer hire but not for Claude Code credits that would ship the same feature faster. Twelve-month plans ossify when the market moves quarterly.
  3. Match-funding excludes the founders the system says it wants to support. Personal-capital match requirements filter out non-traditional founders and increase social inequality at the bottom of the ladder.
  4. Predatory operators in the advice gap. Ratchet term sheets and 50% board-control demands seen from local VCs in NI's recent past. Less common today; the warning persists.
  5. The active bootstrap-vs-raise debate. AI-enabled services founders are deliberately not seeking equity. Angels report the seed-strap model is now more attractive than companies that will raise Series A.
  6. AI is inflating grant-application volume. Same pots, more applications, harder to distinguish signal.

Diagnosis

This is not a money problem. It is a design problem. POC grants that don't pay for AI tooling but will pay for unnecessary developer hires; match-funding that filters out anyone without personal capital; an angel layer that operates entirely on warm introduction and is therefore invisible to most founders; local funds that signal ambition ceilings smaller than the global market would offer the same company. Each of these is fixable without new money.

My ambition as a founder is almost not to raise money through the local funds. Because there's a cap on that. If you're a founder with big ambition the opportunities exist outside Northern Ireland. — Founder, B2B SaaS

techstarsSection 05 · Cultural capital
Warmth, follow-up, fear of failure
05
Cultural capital · #cultural
evidence: high · most consequential quiet capital

The cultural undercurrents are the most consequential capital running quietly in the background. The biggest of them is the one nobody quite said out loud — and it is the most fixable.

Four undercurrents

  1. The ambition trap. Belfast founders calibrate against the company down the road, not the founder in Palo Alto raising on a slide. The Mullaney framing: being the fastest runner in a slow race and mistaking that for winning. This is not a problem of risk-tolerance — the founders in the room work harder, with less, than founders in most cities. It is a problem of benchmark.
  2. Follow-up as a cultural discipline. A network surplus the ecosystem is partly wasting at the user end. Costs zero to fix; cannot be mandated; can be cultivated.
  3. Fear of failure linked to a sibling observation that technology talent is not respected here as it is in San Francisco or London. Starting a company is socially riskier when (a) failure reads as personal failure, and (b) the path is less culturally legitimised than other professional routes.
  4. Bootstrap-as-cultural-statement. A new generation of AI-native founders is making bootstrap a deliberate choice — not a fallback. The angels are increasingly making the same case from the other side. The values of "what success looks like" here are mid-revision.

The good news on ambition. Founder Labs alumni named the New York trip, unprompted, as the moment that recalibrated their ambition. Exposure works. The current weakness is that exposure is occasional, not continuous. Make global exposure infrastructure, not a programme.

What's working

  • Give-first culture: warm intros, real-time mentoring.
  • Safe-space dynamics in dedicated communities.
  • Critique prefaced with gratitude — a healthy ecosystem signal.
  • A room willing to be honest in front of operators — rare.

Where the friction is

  • Ambition benchmarked against the wrong peer group.
  • Pitch-room demographics don't reflect the markets being addressed.
  • No codified founder-protection norms.
  • Narrative concentration on a small number of names.
  • Hesitance to pay advisors with sweat equity, even when right.
Limerick is one third the size of Belfast. It has ten YC companies. We don't. The difference isn't resources. It is which conversations founders are having and who they are pretending to compete with. — Editorial observation

— Founder on raising outside NI

techstarsSection 06 · Institutional capital
The front door problem
06
Institutional capital · #institutional
evidence: high · INI front-door now corroborated independently

Each programme is, individually, well-designed. Together they are illegible. Programmes operate in silos because their KPIs are individual, not collective. And the single largest friction point — Invest NI's front door — could be re-engineered in months, not years.

  1. Too crowded to navigate. Founder Labs, Catalyst, Ormeau Labs, Eagle Labs, Go Succeed, council programmes, university programmes, Innovate UK, Intertrade Ireland, Invest NI. All real. All individually useful. None coherent from a founder's perspective.
  2. Grant criteria assume one shape of company — typically equity-track, product-led.
  3. The Invest NI front door is filtering out the wrong founders. Now corroborated independently across multiple recordings — including from a founder operating successfully in NI for six years who has never gone back. "The form is too long. Invest NI doesn't even know that they do this."
  4. The connection-between-networks problem. A founder rejected by one programme isn't routinely referred onward. KPI structures incentivise individual programmes, not collective coverage.
  5. Public-sector pace. Founders work in weekly sprints. Institutions work in quarters. Decisions wait on annual leave.

Diagnosis

The system around the bright spots is what is failing — not the programmes themselves. Founder Labs, Eagle Labs / Ormeau Labs, Catalyst Invent, Awakened Angels, Go Succeed, Intertrade Ireland were all named, unprompted, as bright spots. The friction is in the connective tissue between them, and in the single front-door interface that decides who gets in.

Recommendation summary. Commission an external review of the Invest NI founder-facing front door with a measurable target. Build a shared cross-referral protocol between programmes. Publish a single navigable map of NI support by stage and sector — resourced as infrastructure, not as a programme.

I went to Invest NI a couple of years ago. It was a lot of forms and time. When you're running a business and you're really busy, is it even worth going? I just walked away from it. — Founder, six years operating in NI

techstarsSection 07 · Human capital
The missing founder mafia
07
Human capital · #human
evidence: high

The most strategically important observation from the day was who was in the room. A new founder generation operating on different unit economics — AI-native, customer-funded, global from week one. The system around them has not yet caught up.

Four lenses

  1. A new founder generation, operating differently. One founder doing the work of a thirty-person team. Domain experts leaving large institutions to build products in those domains. A bootstrapped, customer-funded ethic earlier in the lifecycle. Canadian contracts won by Belfast solo founders before any local agency engagement. This cohort is not a continuation of the cohort before it.
  2. The skills gap — negotiation as a missing capability that founders without accelerator access never fully acquire.
  3. The diversity question — women founders navigating an environment built for someone else; pitch-room asymmetry ("who's the CTO?" — looking for a man even when a woman is the CTO).
  4. The "founder mafia" generational gap — London / NY / SF have generations of large successful companies whose alumni become the next generation's founders, advisors, angels. NI doesn't have this yet. A time problem, not a programme problem — but cultivating the candidates is the work.

What's working

  • Women-only founder communities producing real outcomes.
  • Bank- and corporate-led programmes emerging (Awakened Hub, Awakened Angels, Eagle Lab).
  • Life-sciences university pipeline producing balanced gender representation in spinouts.
  • AI as an upskilling path for non-technical founders — a chartered accountant building her own EMI scheme.

Where the friction is

  • Negotiation acquired by chance, not design.
  • Investment panels demographically narrow.
  • Few female angels and VCs to draw from.
  • No structured exposure to founder as a viable path at school or undergraduate stage.
  • Question-asymmetry on investment panels — a panel-training issue, not just a representation issue.

techstarsSection 08 · Intellectual capital
What AI just changed
08
Intellectual capital · #intellectual
evidence: medium-high · live debate

The founders already using AI properly are operating at a different gear. The institutions in the room are still debating governance and guardrails. The gap between those two states is the structural risk for NI's economy — and the section's central observation.

The FDI exposure

A significant part of NI's FDI base sits in workflows that current-generation AI agents are now demonstrably automating: KYC, AML, legal operations, customer support, BPO. The choice is not whether AI happens to Northern Ireland. It is whether the system is set up to capture the upside or absorb only the downside. The institutional response has, with respect, concentrated on the conversations we cannot move (the model layer, frontier safety, regulation at the level of large jurisdictions) and avoided the conversations we can: whether our grant rules let founders spend on AI tooling, whether our schools and universities teach with AI, whether our programmes are designed for AI-native unit economics.

A live tension worth publishing as a tension

Position A · Investor
"Consultancy businesses are not investable, full stop. Nobody will ever invest in a consultancy."
— Angel investor
Position B · Founder
"As one person I can have six clients, where before that would have needed a team of thirty or forty. The cap on what you can earn isn't quite the way it used to be."
— Founder, AI-native services

The angel's position reflects the historical model: linear time-for-money. The founder's reflects what AI has changed: productised services with software-like margins. The ecosystem has not yet developed shared language to distinguish "old-style consultancy" from "AI-native services". That language gap is itself the intellectual-capital observation.

techstarsSection 09 · Physical capital
The absence is the data
09
Physical capital · #physical
evidence: minimal · finding is firm

For three full hours of recorded conversation, no founder talked about Belfast as a place. That's not because the place isn't an asset — it's because the place is taken for granted. A startup community that has stopped seeing its own backdrop has lost a narrative tool.

Founders did not talk about Belfast as a place, didn't reference physical hubs as differentiators, didn't discuss density or co-location as advantages — even when prompted by a facilitator who explicitly raised "density" as a feature of NI.

The closest physical-capital moment was incidental: a founder mentioning they chose Belfast over Dublin because it was "a lot more affordable than Dublin". A quiet positive — surfaced as one sentence, in passing, not as a frame.

Feld and Hathaway specifically argue that physical capital — quality of place, density, infrastructure — runs in the background of the most successful startup communities and is often underestimated. Boulder's identity as a place is, in their writing, inseparable from its identity as a startup community.

What the absence could mean

  • Physical capital genuinely isn't a constraint or differentiator — in which case it's also not a story to tell.
  • Physical capital is taken for granted — in which case it's working but undervalued as narrative.
  • Founders here haven't yet learned to use place as part of their story — in which case there's a missed narrative opportunity.

The third hypothesis is the most useful. Belfast has real physical-capital assets — Ormeau Labs, Catalyst Inc, university corridors, walkable density, affordability vs Dublin / London, ease of cross-border travel — that are not being deployed as part of NI's startup-community story. As one founder put it in passing: "in a major hub you compete for everything — talent, attention, runway. Here the math is different."

Deliberately surface physical-capital narratives in ecosystem storytelling: affordability vs Dublin, compactness as networking advantage, hub density (Ormeau, Catalyst, university corridors), ease of cross-border travel and work.

techstarsSection 10 · Cross-cutting
Asymmetry & the connected core
10
Cross-cutting observations
patterns running across capitals

The meta-finding: information asymmetry between connected and unconnected founders.

Almost every friction in the dataset can be reframed as an asymmetry problem. The ecosystem's strongest interventions all close this asymmetry for founders inside them. The opportunity is to extend the same protection to founders outside.

Eight patterns that run across capitals

  1. Asymmetry is the meta-finding. Connected founders compound; unconnected ones get filtered out.
  2. NI is rich in two capitals (network, cultural-warmth) and short in two (institutional-legibility, codified culture). The leverage move is to spend the surplus on the deficit.
  3. Founders trust each other more than formal channels. Formal infrastructure sets the conditions; the value transfer happens between founders.
  4. The most experienced founders deliberately limit their exposure to "general" ecosystem activity — small, trusted, online over big, broad, in-person.
  5. Tone: gratitude with honest critique. The ecosystem is being asked to finish the job, not torn down.
  6. The follow-up culture problem — costs zero to fix at founder level. Cannot be mandated; can be cultivated.
  7. The bootstrap-vs-raise debate is no longer fringe. Treat it as live.
  8. The shockwave is yet to come, but the candidates are visible. Cultivate exit alumni before they dissolve into private life.

Recommendations
ranked by frequency × force × actionability

Synthesised from founder suggestions and the author's analysis. Five priorities sit at the top — chosen because none require substantial new money, all are implementable, and together they constitute the leverage move: spend NI's surplus network and cultural capital on its deficit of institutional and human capital.

The five priorities, in order of leverage
  1. Reform grants to reward velocity and customers — not application-writing. AI tooling eligible spend; drop personal match-funding pre-revenue.
  2. Fix the Invest NI front door — an external review with a measurable target. Months, not years.
  3. Build the missing peer network — a working channel of 200+ NI founders across stages, anchored jointly across Catalyst / Founder Labs / Ormeau Labs.

What each stakeholder can do
specific · sequenced · doable this year

This is the ask. Five stakeholder groups; one report; specific moves. Indigenous founder density is the conversion mechanism between AI-era productivity and NI's GDP — and that mechanism only works if each of these groups operates differently. None of these asks requires substantial new money. All require honesty about how we operate.

Founders
The people the report is about
  • Stop benchmarking against the company down the road.
  • Plan for external capital from week one.
  • Get on a plane.
  • Apply to YC even if you don't get in.
  • Use AI as an operating model, not a feature.
  • Charge for the MVP.
  • Refuse the term sheet with 50% board seats.
  • When you raise, introduce two founders behind you.
Universities
Queen's, Ulster, AFBI, FE
  • Treat founder formation as core economic infrastructure.
  • Build a working spinout pipeline the founder community can see and engage with.
  • Make student entrepreneurs visible to investors and to each other earlier.
  • Integrate AI fluency into curriculum design, urgently.
  • Open access to research and IP commercialisation as continuous flow, not a once-a-year event.
Government
DfE · INI · councils · BBB
  • Reform grants to reward velocity and customers, not application-writing.
  • Fix the Invest NI front door.
  • Resource the founder peer network as infrastructure.
  • Make global exposure continuous.
  • Take a public position that NI builds with AI.
  • Treat indigenous founder density as economic policy, not as a CSR programme.
Highest leverage
Investors
VCs, angels, UK / Irish capital
  • Operate at the speed founders need, not the speed legacy fund structures allow.
  • Be honest about local capital ceilings; route founders externally when you can't match the round.
  • Open the angel layer to public criteria and public deal flow.
  • Co-invest with London and Dublin rather than competing against them.
  • Treat founder education as part of the deal, not an aftermarket service.
Corporates
NI-HQ businesses & FDI
  • Engage as customers, not sponsors.
  • Open procurement to local startups in a structured way.
  • Move talent — second-time founders need a path back into corporate-funded roles and out again.
  • Treat the founder economy as your innovation pipeline, not your community-relations budget.

The case for Techstars Belfast
the explicit close · author's position
The thesis, restated

If indigenous founder density is the conversion mechanism between AI productivity and NI GDP, the connective tissue between founders, capital, programmes, universities, government, and corporates needs to be owned by someone.

Today it is not. Each institutional body operates inside its own KPI structure. The founder economy lives in the gaps between them. That is precisely where the asymmetry described in Section 10 actually compounds — and precisely where the recommendations in this report stand or fall.

Why Techstars Belfast can hold these threads

  • Independent of any single institutional body. Equally credible to all five stakeholder groups, captive to none.
  • Inside the global Techstars network. Access founders here cannot otherwise reach — programmatic, not on personal hustle.
  • Physically inside Ormeau Labs. Density advantage built in.
  • AI-native in our own operating model. Lets us run at the speed founders now need.
  • The room was willing to be honest in front of us. On the basis of 6 May, this is the operator the founder economy is willing to tell the truth to.

The explicit ask

Read the report. Engage with the recommendations. And help us build the entity that converts the founder economy into GDP movement for Northern Ireland.

Several of the recommendations in Section 11 will be implemented by Techstars Belfast directly — the peer network, the diaspora bridge, the continuous global-exposure programme. Others require institutional partners — the front-door redesign, grant reform, the public AI posture. All of them benefit when the connective tissue between groups is funded and resourced.

This document establishes the case. The conversations that follow are where investment in Techstars Belfast as that entity gets agreed.

Indigenous founder density is one of the highest-leverage available interventions for raising NI's GDP. Every recommendation in this document is sequenced against that thesis. — The thesis of the report

Open offers & what happens next
an invitation, not a verdict

Eleven questions this draft raises but doesn't resolve.

  1. Who would own the public "founder best practice" guide? Founder Labs? Catalyst? Invest NI? A founder-collective? Awaken Hub? The answer matters because the guide's credibility is in who writes it.
  2. Is the "geography penalty" a fact, a perception, or both? Worth digging into actual term-sheet data alongside founder testimony before publishing as fact.
  3. Why are women-founder programmes so infrequent? Funding constraint, demand misjudgement, or operator capacity? "She Ventures" runs every two years explicitly because of operational lift — that suggests capacity, not demand.
  4. Why is physical capital so absent from these conversations? Now firm finding across three recordings: taken for granted, not viewed as narrative asset.
  5. What's the right unit of "ecosystem map"? Geographic, stage-based, sector-based, founder-type-based?
  6. How replicable is the AI-enabled services model? A small handful of unusually capable founders, or a structural shift in what "scalable" means?
  7. What do founders we didn't hear from think? The sharpest critique would come from founders who never made it in — or who left NI to build elsewhere.
  8. Is there a "tier 2" ecosystem city playbook? Limerick (population 100k, 10 YC companies) is the comparator that most demands attention.
  9. Who would convene the persistent founder peer infrastructure? Catalyst, Founder Labs, and Ormeau Labs all have a credible claim.
  10. What does "investable AI-native services" look like to a sophisticated investor? The "consultancy is not investable / I'm at £400k week five" tension is the biggest unresolved intellectual-capital question. A fireside was offered live at the table — honour the offer.
  11. What is the actual rate of warm-intro follow-up by NI founders, and how does it compare? Empirically. Senior operators perceive the rate as low. Worth measuring.
Live offers made on the record
  • A fireside chat between an angel investor and an AI-native services founder, on the record.
  • Warm introductions to specific INI contacts to fix the front-door experience.
  • A follow-up coffee on machine learning between founders.

These are commitments the ecosystem made to itself, in public. The next pass of this report will track which ones happened.

The ecosystem is not being attacked here. It's being asked to finish the job. — Editorial close
